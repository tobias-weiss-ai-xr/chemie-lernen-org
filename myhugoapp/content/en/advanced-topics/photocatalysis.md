# Photocatalysis

## Overview

Photocatalysis uses light to activate a catalyst, driving chemical reactions that would not proceed under thermal conditions alone. When a semiconductor absorbs photons with energy equal to or greater than its bandgap, electrons are excited from the valence band to the conduction band, leaving behind positively charged holes. These photogenerated charge carriers migrate to the surface and participate in redox reactions with adsorbed molecules.

The field began in earnest after Akira Fujishima and Kenichi Honda reported photocatalytic water splitting on a TiO₂ electrode under UV light in 1972. Since then, photocatalysis has expanded to environmental remediation (degrading pollutants), self-cleaning surfaces, organic synthesis, and, most importantly, solar fuel production. The ability to store solar energy in chemical bonds, converting sunlight and water into hydrogen fuel, is one of the grand challenges of chemistry.

## Key Concepts

- **Semiconductor Band Structure** — A photocatalyst needs a conduction band energy level more negative than the reduction potential of the target reaction, and a valence band more positive than the oxidation potential. For water splitting, the conduction band must be more negative than the H⁺/H₂ potential (0 V vs. NHE), and the valence band more positive than the O₂/H₂O potential (+1.23 V vs. NHE). TiO₂ (bandgap 3.2 eV for anatase) meets these thermodynamic requirements but only absorbs UV light, which is less than 5% of solar energy.

- **Charge Separation and Recombination** — After photoexcitation, the electron-hole pair must reach the catalyst surface before recombining. This is the central kinetic challenge. Recombination can be radiative (emitting light) or non-radiative (releasing heat). Strategies to suppress recombination include nanostructuring (shorter diffusion paths), heterojunctions (spatial charge separation), and cocatalysts (selective charge extraction at the surface).

- **Water Splitting** — The overall reaction is $2\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2$ with $\Delta G = +237$ kJ/mol. This is a thermodynamically uphill process, which is exactly what makes it useful: the products (H₂ and O₂) store the solar energy input. The hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER) each require specific catalyst surfaces. Platinum is the benchmark HER catalyst; IrO₂ and RuO₂ are benchmark OER catalysts.

- **Visible Light Photocatalysts** — Since UV light is a small fraction of sunlight, researchers have developed visible-light photocatalysts with narrower bandgaps. Examples include WO₃ (2.7 eV), Fe₂O₃ (2.1 eV), g-C₃N₄ (2.7 eV), and BiVO₄ (2.4 eV). Doping, defect engineering, and solid solutions (like (Ga₁₋ₓZnₓ)(N₁₋ₓOₓ)) further tune the bandgap and band edge positions.

- **Dye-Sensitized Systems** — Molecular dyes can absorb visible light and inject electrons into a wide-bandgap semiconductor like TiO₂. This decouples light absorption from charge transport, mimicking natural photosynthesis. Ruthenium polypyridyl complexes (N3, N719 dyes) and, increasingly, organic dyes and perovskites serve as sensitizers.

## Applications

**Solar hydrogen production** is the ultimate goal. Devices using modified TiO₂, Ta₃N₅, or SrTiO₃ with cocatalysts achieve solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiencies of 1-2% under real sunlight. The target for commercial viability is around 10% STH, meaning 10% of incoming solar energy is stored as hydrogen. Recent work on Al-doped SrTiO₃ and Rh-doped GaN:ZnO solid solutions has pushed STH above 1% for overall water splitting without sacrificial agents.

**Environmental remediation** uses TiO₂ to degrade organic pollutants, bacteria, and viruses in air and water. Self-cleaning glass, antibacterial tiles, and air purification systems all rely on photocatalytic oxidation. The reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by illuminated TiO₂, particularly hydroxyl radicals (·OH), are powerful oxidants that break down most organic contaminants.

**Photocatalytic organic synthesis** offers greener reaction pathways. Photocatalysts can drive C-H functionalization, cross-coupling, and oxidation reactions under mild conditions. Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C₃N₄) and organic photocatalysts like eosin Y have replaced heavy metal catalysts in several reaction classes, aligning with green chemistry principles.

## Formulas and Equations

The **overall water splitting** reaction:

$$2\text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{h\nu} 2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2 \quad \Delta G = +237.2 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

This requires photons with energy exceeding 1.23 eV per electron transferred (4 electrons in total), corresponding to wavelengths shorter than about 1000 nm. Practical systems need higher-energy photons due to overpotentials.

The **bandgap requirement** for water splitting:

$$E_g \geq 1.23 \text{ eV} + E_{\text{overpotential}} \approx 1.6 \text{–} 2.0 \text{ eV}$$

The **rate of photocatalytic reaction** depends on the quantum efficiency $\Phi$:

$$r = \frac{\Phi \cdot I_0 \cdot A}{N_A}$$

where $I_0$ is the incident photon flux, $A$ is the illuminated area, and $N_A$ is Avogadro's number.

The **apparent quantum yield (AQY)** at a specific wavelength:

$$\text{AQY}(\lambda) = \frac{2 \times \text{moles of H}_2 \text{ evolved}}{\text{moles of incident photons at } \lambda} \times 100\%$$

The factor of 2 accounts for the two electrons needed per H₂ molecule.

The **solar-to-hydrogen (STH) efficiency**:

$$\text{STH} = \frac{\text{energy content of produced H}_2}{\text{energy of incident sunlight}} = \frac{R_{\text{H}_2} \times \Delta G^\circ}{P_{\text{solar}} \times A} \times 100\%$$

where $R_{\text{H}_2}$ is the hydrogen production rate, $\Delta G^\circ = 237$ kJ/mol, and $P_{\text{solar}}$ is the solar irradiance.
